The problem with Robin's argument is not the mere fact that we people can't visit factory farms, but the reasons that explain this situation. If factory farms were closed to visitors due to e.g. health concerns, that would be a problem for Robin only in the sense that his argument asks readers to do something that they cannot possibly do. But the fact that ordinary individuals aren't allowed inside factory farms due instead to fears that they won't like what they see constitutes a different kind of problem. This is strong evidence that the conditions inside factory farms are actually the sort

More generally, I think the following principle has prima facie validity: "If acquiring more information about x is at least somewhat likely to make you form a bad opinion about y, then if x is actively preventing you from acquiring that information, you are warranted in concluding that you would have formed a bad opinion about y had you been allowed to acquire that information.

Examples: Factory farming; Iraq War; Argentine inflation
